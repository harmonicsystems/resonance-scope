Calibration read-outs

A descriptive baseline from your /i a u/ anchors, set when you calibrate (it won't change until you recalibrate) — characterizes your own vowel space, not diagnostic.

Honest limits. F1/F2 here come from peak-picking a smoothed spectrum — a genuine first-pass estimate, not clinical LPC, and it can confuse a strong harmonic for a formant on high or breathy notes. Read the movement and clustering, not the absolute Hz. Points drawn hollow fell back to a plain-max pick — a low-confidence estimate, worth extra caution. And nasality stays this method's blind spot: the velum's signature is missing energy (anti-formants), which a spectrogram shows poorly — that axis wants a nasometer, not a mic.

What the camera can and can't see. The mouth score reads only two visible dials — jaw aperture and lip spread — normalized to your face so it's relative, not absolute. Tongue frontness, lip protrusion (depth), and everything inside the tract stay invisible to a camera, exactly as nasality stays invisible to the mic. It's a partial, complementary hit-rate, not a fuller or "more correct" measurement — and it never grades your shape against a target.

About calibration. Anchoring to your own /i a u/ turns raw Hz into a position inside your own vowel space — that's what makes clustering stable session-to-session and comparable across different vocal tracts. Two honest caveats: the normalized space is only as good as the anchors, so capture each corner while the steadiness meter is high; and normalization fixes the scale, not the underlying estimate — F1/F2 are still peak-picked, not LPC. The read-outs (VSA, centralization) describe your own space and are never a diagnosis.

About F0 (the source). F0 is the source — vocal-fold rate — shown in warm, distinct from the cool filter (F1/F2). It's a first-pass estimate too: it can octave-halve or double on breathy or creaky phonation. Unvoiced and whispered sounds have no F0, so a blank read-out is correct, not a failure. And in a cluster, F0 drift is a pitch-steadiness check — it tells you whether you held pitch while varying the vowel — not a reproducibility score; high drift is a caution that a wide F1/F2 cluster may just be pitch wobble.
